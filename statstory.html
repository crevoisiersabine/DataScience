As a demonstration of the application of bayesian statistical analysis we attempt to answer the following question.

Did our working habits change over the 6 months of studying ?


In order to answer this question we model our working hours each day as random variables drawn from a distribution. The above question can therefore be re-phrased as: does the distribution change significantly over the 6 months of studying. In order to answer this, we attempt to fit a number of different distributions to the dataset, aligned with our belief that at certain points during the 6 months, the distribution changes.

This approach is common to many Bayesian problems and involves first defining our prior belief then updating it based upon our dataset. Often it is extremely difficult or not possible to arrive at a mathematically precise description of the posterior distribution (the distribution once the data has been accounted for), however using MCMC (Monte Carlo Markov Chain) simulations we can explore this posterior distribution and gain an understanding of its most important regions.

This page cannot fully cover the methods of Bayesian Statistics but please refer to the accompanying ipython notebook and excellent starting point Bayesian Methods for Hackers for further details.

Below is our daily data on total hours worked. It is this from this data that we will try to infer if there has been a change at some point in the number of hours worked.

Figure 1: Hours Worked over 6 months

We model the data as a Poisson distribution with parameter lambda; this makes sense as the Poisson distribution deals with discrete count data. The rate parameter defining the Poisson distribution is what we believe may have changed during this period, possibly getting lower at some point in time.

We do not know what value lambda takes, however we know it is a positive real number, and therefore choose to model it as an exponential distribution with hyper-parameter alpha. Alpha is chosen to be the inverse of the average value of the hours worked, as a prior.

The point(s) where we believe a shift in study behaviour has occurred is defined by tau. It appears that in our dataset there are four separate time periods, however we do not know when a shift occurred within those periods or even that a shift occurred at all. Therefore we define three uniform time distributions covering these specific periods where we believe a different study dynamic may have been established. Within each, we search for a switch point.

Below are the posterior distributions resulting from our MCMC simulation which updated our prior beliefs based on our dataset. On the left hand side are our distributions of lambda (defining the Poisson distribution and hours worked) and on the right is our tau distribution (definining the change point in time).

Figure 2: Posterior distributions of lambda and tau parameters

A simple approach to inference is to consider the most probable range of parameter values using the parameter's posterior distribution. Below are the Highest Posterior Density (HPD) regions for the lambda parameters of our Poisson distributio. The HPD in this case defines the region within which we have a 95% probability of the true value existing.

Figure 3: Highest Posterior Density regions

We can clearly see that the analysis has identified the holiday period, highlighted in green, where only an estimated 0-1h of studying has been carried out each day.

The time periods directly preceding and directly following the holiday period, shown in the red and purple colours, aren't very distinct in hours worked and their distributions largely overlap. This seems to indicate there is not a significant change in studying behaviour between these two periods.

However there seems to be a change in behaviour that gets established at the end, as shown by the blue distribution. The tau_c variable suggests that the most likely day for this change happened around day 75. Prior to day 75, outside of the holiday period, we seemed to be working around 7h days, whereas from day 75 onwards, we seemed to work around 5h days; this is an average, and as this dataset includes all breaks, it is probably an overstated hour drop.
